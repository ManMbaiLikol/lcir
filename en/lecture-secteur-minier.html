Mining sector: Cameroon aims to regain control of its subsoil

By Man Mbaï Likol·16 July 2026

Between the launch of major industrial projects, the Cameroonisation of gold titles and tighter environmental and fiscal rules, the government is outlining a new mining policy.

The stakes go beyond opening new mines: the question is whether Cameroon can convert its geological wealth into industrial capacity, public revenue, skilled jobs and national power.

The acting Minister of Mines, Industry and Technological Development, Professor Fuh Calistus Gentry, and the Minister of Communication, René Emmanuel Sadi, held a joint press briefing in Yaoundé devoted to the "new face of Cameroon's mining sector, with the launch of major projects and the restructuring of the gold sector."

Held at the auditorium of the Ministry of Communication, this government address aimed to present the progress made in developing major mining projects, but above all the measures undertaken to regain control of a gold sector long marked by informality, weak traceability of output, fiscal losses and environmental damage.

Beyond the technical announcements, this briefing raises a fundamental question: is Cameroon turning its geological potential into a genuine instrument of economic sovereignty?

Five projects to open a new mining sequence

According to MINMIDT, 2025 marked a historic turning point, with Cameroon gradually moving from a country holding significant mineral reserves to one engaged in industrial production. Five structuring projects were highlighted:

  • the Bipindi–Grand-Zambi iron mining project;
  • the Kribi-Lobé iron mining project;
  • the Minim-Martap bauxite project, in the Adamawa region;
  • the industrial marble project at Bidzar;
  • the Colomine gold project.

The government presents these projects as the first pillars of a new Cameroonian mining cycle. The stakes, however, go beyond simply opening mines. Bringing these deposits into production must drive the construction of rail, port, road, energy and industrial infrastructure capable of durably structuring the territories concerned.

The iron of Bipindi–Grand-Zambi and Kribi-Lobé can strengthen the southern industrial and port corridor. The bauxite of Minim-Martap can help bring about a national aluminium chain — provided Cameroon does not limit itself to exporting raw ore. The marble of Bidzar can support national construction and materials-processing industries. As for the gold of Colomine, it raises directly the question of traceability, taxation and the possible building of national reserves.

Gold, symbol of the old model's weaknesses

Restructuring the gold sector is the other major thrust of the government's message. For many years, a large share of semi-mechanised artisanal mining developed in an insufficiently controlled environment: titles lent or hidden behind front men, under-declared output, gold sold through informal channels, sites abandoned without rehabilitation, and public revenue below the sector's real potential.

The reform presented by MINMIDT therefore aims to restore the State's authority over the entire chain: granting authorisations, identifying owners, controlling production, paying taxes, processing ore and restoring sites.

An environmental bond of 63 million CFA francs

The first safeguard concerns environmental protection. Each semi-mechanised artisanal mining site must now be covered by an environmental bond announced at 63 million CFA francs. This sum is meant to serve as a guarantee for the restoration and rehabilitation of degraded areas.

This obligation responds to a long-neglected reality: a mine does not only produce revenue and ore. It can also leave behind unusable land, polluted watercourses, dangerous excavations and communities deprived of their traditional means of subsistence.

Sovereignty is therefore not only about extracting a national resource. It also means not durably sacrificing the national territory for an immediate economic gain.

At least 51% of capital held by Cameroonians

The second measure concerns what the government calls the "nationalisation of titles." In legal and economic terms, however, it would be more accurate to speak of the Cameroonisation of capital. To obtain a semi-mechanised artisanal mining authorisation, a company must be at least 51% owned by one or more Cameroonians.

The measure aims to prevent foreign operators from indirectly controlling authorisations reserved for nationals through front arrangements. But its effectiveness will depend on the administration's ability to identify companies' true beneficial owners.

A Cameroonian stake written on paper is not enough if the financing, decisions, equipment, marketing and profits remain entirely controlled from abroad. Sovereignty requires real Cameroonian shareholders with effective economic power — not mere administrative front men.

Taxation and minimum production thresholds

The new framework provides for taxation presented as non-negotiable:

  • 25% under the flat-rate mining income tax (impôt synthétique minier libératoire);
  • 5% for export duties;
  • 1% for the mining-policy implementation funds.

Minimum monthly production thresholds are also announced:

  • 5 kilograms of gold for a site equipped with 15 bôles;
  • 7 kilograms for 20 bôles;
  • 10 kilograms for 30 bôles.

These thresholds are meant to let the State compare installed capacity with officially declared quantities. They thus provide a tool against under-declaration and the clandestine outflow of gold.

But controls will have to go further: certified weighing, numbering of output, a digital registry, geolocation of sites, identification of buyers, export controls and regular reconciliation between declared production, taxes paid and volumes sold.

For a State that does not precisely know how much gold is extracted from its territory cannot claim to fully exercise its mining sovereignty.

Technology serving control and the environment

Operators also have six months to migrate to closed-circuit systems for processing mineralised gravel, notably through controlled leaching.

The stated objective is to limit uncontrolled discharges, improve ore recovery and better regulate the use of chemical substances. This modernisation must be accompanied by regular inspections, traceability mechanisms and effective penalties.

Technology, however, must not become a mere administrative criterion. It must help reduce ore losses, protect workers, limit soil and water contamination, and make production more easily verifiable by State services.

A mine becomes sovereign only when it develops the country

Opening major projects and tightening the rules is progress, but it is not yet enough to define a sovereign mining policy.

A country can extract a great deal of ore while remaining economically dependent. This is notably the case when raw materials are exported without processing, when equipment is entirely imported, when financing comes exclusively from abroad and when most of the profits leave the territory.

Mining sovereignty must therefore be assessed through several concrete outcomes:

  • the share of ore processed locally;
  • the skilled jobs created for Cameroonians;
  • the national companies integrated into subcontracting;
  • the infrastructure built and shared with the territories;
  • the revenue actually paid to the State and local authorities;
  • the real participation of nationals in the capital;
  • the transfer of technology and skills;
  • the protection of neighbouring communities;
  • the effective restoration of mined sites;
  • Cameroon's ability to know, certify and market its own resources.

The real battle for sovereignty is therefore not about closing the sector to foreign capital. It is about imposing on investment an architecture compatible with Cameroon's strategic interests.

From the subsoil to national power

The joint MINMIDT–MINCOM briefing reveals a will to change: a shift to industrial mining, greater control of gold, Cameroonisation of authorisations, stronger taxation and environmental accountability for operators.

This direction deserves support, but also scrutiny. For even the most ambitious texts produce sovereignty only when they are applied transparently and consistently.

Cameroon must no longer be merely the legal owner of its subsoil. It must become the master of geological knowledge, of the granting of titles, of production control, of industrial processing and of the allocation of revenue.

The Cameroonian mine will be truly sovereign only when every tonne of iron, bauxite or marble, and every kilogram of gold extracted, helps build infrastructure, finance the State, develop national skills and prepare the country's industrial future.

"The subsoil is a potential. Only organisation, local processing and national control can turn it into power."
